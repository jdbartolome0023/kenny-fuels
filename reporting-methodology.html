How to Read the Numbers in Every Kenny Fuels Report

A single, generic reference for how attribution, verification, and real profit are calculated across every Google Ads and Meta Ads report, for any period. This page doesn't change period to period the way the weekly reports do; it explains the method behind them once, so the numbers stay auditable rather than just asserted. Linked from every report, not rewritten for each one.

The Core Problem
What Ad Platforms Report Isn't What Happened

Every ad platform (Google, Meta) tracks conversions with its own pixel or tag, fired in the visitor's browser or via server-side API. That tracking depends on the visitor accepting cookies, and increasingly on statistical modeling to fill gaps when they don't. WooCommerce's own order data is the one source that's independent of any ad platform's self-reporting, it only records what actually got purchased. Every report compares the two.

🔒 Why they diverge: a platform's self-reported number can include purchases from visitors who declined tracking consent (statistically modeled, not observed), visitors on a different device than the one that clicked the ad, and in Google's case specifically, sometimes a technical misconfiguration on top. WooCommerce's own order data has no such inflation, but it can undercount too, since its own attribution capture has real limits (see Verification Tiers below).
The Method
Reporting a Floor and a Ceiling, Not a Guess

Whenever a platform's claimed number and WooCommerce's verified number disagree, the report shows both as a floor-to-ceiling range rather than picking one. Numbers below are illustrative of the method, not this period's actual figures, see the live weekly report for current numbers.

⚖️ Neither end is "the answer" on its own. The floor is provably real but almost certainly too low: it excludes every consent-declining or cross-device visitor a platform may have genuinely influenced. The ceiling is the platform's own number, known to include some traffic it didn't verifiably drive. Reporting only the ceiling overstates performance; reporting only the floor understates it. Showing both is the honest answer until something narrows the range.
Reading The Reports
Three Tiers of Confidence, Not Two

Every figure in a Kenny Fuels report carries one of these three labels.

VERIFIED

Comes from WooCommerce's own order data, independent of any ad platform. The most trustworthy figure available, treated as the floor. Still likely an undercount, since WooCommerce's own attribution capture has the same consent limits, plus its own separate technical gaps under active investigation.

PLAUSIBLE SIGNAL

A secondary check, e.g. cross-referencing orders tagged to another channel against product categories a given platform doesn't advertise at all. Suggestive of undercounted attribution, never proof. Shown separately, never merged into the verified floor.

SELF-REPORTED

Comes from the platform's own dashboard. Useful for comparing that platform's campaigns against each other (it's the only figure with per-campaign resolution when WooCommerce can't confirm a specific campaign), but known to overstate true results, so it should never stand alone as "the" profit number.

A Real Limit, Worked Around Where Possible
Verifying Down to the Campaign Level

WooCommerce's attribution usually only identifies the channel (e.g. "Meta"), not which specific campaign within it. But when a platform's own campaign name gets captured in the order's attribution tag, that order can be matched to one specific campaign, giving a real, if small, verified data point where one wouldn't otherwise exist.

💡 Worked example, from the Jul 1-12, 2026 report: of 6 WooCommerce-verified Meta orders that period, 3 carried a specific campaign name in their attribution tag (Compost x2, Firewood B2B x1); the other 3 only showed a generic "Paid Social" tag with no campaign identified. That gave real verified evidence for 2 of the account's 7 campaigns that period, and zero for the other 5, information the account-wide floor/ceiling range alone doesn't surface. Recommendations should treat those 5 zero-verification campaigns with real caution regardless of how strong their self-reported numbers look.

This only works when it works: most Meta-attributed orders don't carry a campaign name, so most periods will still have far more "self-reported only" campaigns than verified ones. Report this honestly rather than implying every campaign has been checked.

A Secondary Check
The Category Cross-Reference Technique

A way to surface plausibly-undercounted attribution without claiming false certainty: check whether orders tagged to one channel (or to no channel at all) are in product categories that another platform doesn't advertise.

🔍 Worked example, from the Jul 1-12, 2026 report: Compost and Sand+Stones are Meta-only categories, Google Ads doesn't run any campaign for them. 22 orders tagged Google/Organic/Direct that period were in those categories, worth €3,165.57, none counted as Meta-attributed anywhere. This could be genuine Meta-influenced revenue that converted later through a different last-click channel, or it could be organic demand with no Meta involvement at all: not distinguishable from order data alone. Reported as a separate, clearly-labeled PLAUSIBLE SIGNAL, never merged into the verified floor.

Worth repeating every period as a standing check, not a one-off. The specific categories and dollar amounts will change; the technique doesn't.

The Missing Piece, Now Resolved
From Revenue to Real Profit

Revenue isn't profit. Kenny Fuels runs on tight margins, so a strong ROAS can still be a real loss once the actual cost of the product is factored in. Ciaran supplied approximate gross margin % by category on Jul 8, 2026, applied to every report since.

💡 Worked example, illustrative margins only: a campaign generates €6,200 claimed revenue on €687 spend (9.0x ROAS, looks like the best campaign in the account). At a 15% margin, gross profit is €930, minus the €687 spend leaves €243 real profit. At an 8% margin, gross profit is €496, which is less than the €687 spent, a loss, despite the identical 9x ROAS. The margin percentage is what decides which of those two stories is true, which is why ROAS alone is never used to justify a spend decision in any report.

Margin applies to every tier: the verified floor, the plausible signal, and the platform's self-reported ceiling alike. It narrows "was this profitable" from a revenue question into an actual profit range, campaign by campaign, but it does not close the attribution gap itself, that's a separate problem addressed below.

One Important Difference
Google's Gap and Meta's Gap Are Not the Same Problem

Both reports use the range method above, but for different reasons, and with different expected outcomes. Don't assume what fixes one will fix the other.

Meta: permanent, by law

  • Graphedia confirmed (Jul 7, 2026): visitors who decline marketing cookies cannot legally be tracked back to a Meta ad, by any method, including server-side Conversions API, under Irish ePrivacy/GDPR rules.
  • This is not an in-progress fix. The gap narrows only for the subset of visitors who do consent, never for those who decline.
  • Report language should never imply this is temporary or "being resolved."

Google: fixable, currently broken

  • Google Ads' own consent handling (Consent Mode v2) is a different, less restrictive mechanism than Meta's: it uses anonymized statistical modeling rather than requiring identity-level matching, so Google's gap has historically run much smaller than Meta's.
  • Josette applied a UTM tagging fix to the Google Ads account Jul 8, 2026 (a Final URL suffix added via the Google Ads API, no website access needed) to let WooCommerce cleanly separate Google Ads clicks from other Google-referred traffic. As of Jul 13, 2026, that fix is confirmed correctly configured on the Google Ads side but not yet showing any effect in WooCommerce's own order data. Since the cause sits on the website side (WooCommerce settings, Complianz, or GTM, none of which Josette has access to beyond Analytics), the follow-up investigation was sent to Graphedia.
  • Once resolved, this gap should close for real, unlike Meta's. Until then, treat it with the same floor/ceiling caution.
⚠️ Don't port language between the two reports out of habit. A phrase like "expected to narrow" is only ever true for Google's fixable issue, never for Meta's. A phrase like "permanent limitation" is only ever true for Meta's, never for Google's, unless a future finding proves otherwise.
In Practice
How This Changes What Gets Reported
01

Every "was it profitable" question gets a range answer

Never a single figure where a platform/WooCommerce gap exists. Floor (verified) and ceiling (platform's claim), both shown, both labelled.

02

Per-campaign recommendations get a confidence check, not just a number

Before ranking campaigns by self-reported profit, check whether WooCommerce can verify any real order for each one. Campaigns with zero verified orders in a period get held, not recommended for a budget increase, regardless of how strong their self-reported number looks.

03

The permanent-limitation disclaimer stays current, and stays Meta-specific

Never implies Meta's consent-tracking gap is "in progress" or "being fixed." It isn't, and won't be, per Graphedia's Jul 7, 2026 confirmation. Google's separate, currently-broken-but-fixable tracking issue gets its own honest status update instead, not this language.

04

Margin data is applied, but its own confidence gaps stay flagged

Category margins are live (received Jul 8, 2026). Two figures (Brand Search 14.6%, BOF Retargeting 22.5%) are still derived estimates, not Ciaran's direct numbers: every report using them carries an explicit disclaimer distinguishing them from the seven confirmed category margins.

05

This page updates in place as the method changes

If a tracking gap closes, if a new verification technique gets adopted, or if margin data changes, this reference gets updated here rather than re-explained inside each period's report.
